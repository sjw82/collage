General Themes

All of the following artistic movements reject tradition and operate on their own terms, as nebulously defined as they may be. My intention is to create a multi paneled collage with different panels drawing on different time periods of art history. Each of the following movements build on the preceding creating a mosaic of reactionary artistic commentary. I primarily work in the neo-dadaist style, so it will be a challenge to research and create in several new genres.

Dadaism

Time Period: 1916-1924

Key Words: absurdist, irrational, anti-art, found-object

Catalyzed by World War I and spurned by the work of Freud, Einstein, and Marx, Dadaism started in Europe and lasted about a decade. In reaction to the atrocities of war, the Dada movement embodied absurdist philosophy—the idea that human life is meaningless and chaotic, but not pointless. The primary focus of Dadaism was to challenge conventions and upend the system of art. It was the first movement that was not centered on creating aesthetically pleasing images but instead was “anti-art” in that it rejected traditional ideas of beauty and originality. Much Dadaist art was found-object rather than originally produced. It was in this period that collage became popular as imagery and language was recycled. The movement was decidedly anti-war and often satirized war propaganda and journalism by creating new, often contradictory, meanings using their words in collages.

Surrealism

Time Period: 1920s-1930s

Key Words: psychoanalysis, imagination, surreality

Surrealism, under the influence of Freudian psychology, focused on unlocking the imagination through the unconscious mind; as a result the art often looks dreamy and outlandish. While it does not focus on irrationality like the Dada movement, Surrealism does react against reason. Rationalism was overtaking Europe in this time period and Surrealists wanted to reinvigorate creativity and imagination to create “an absolute reality, a surreality.” (Breton, The Surrealist Manifesto) Because the art is closely tied to the unconscious of the artist, it is difficult to generalism the imagery or style conventions..

Avant Garde and Modernism

Time Period: 1850s-1950s

Key Words: innovation, experimentation

Modernism and by extension Avant Garde was defined by its push for experimentation. New techniques and content drove these movements for over 150 years. Modernism reacted against Victorian morality and industrialization. Avant Garde, a later phase of the movement, includes Impressionism, Post-Impressionism, Cubism, Futurism, Expressionism, Constructivism, de Stijl, and Abstract Expressionism. A greater focus was placed on what was inherent to the form: color, line, form, etc.

Postmodernism

Time Period: 1950s-Present

Key Words: low and high culture, inauthenticity, spectacle

Postmoderism is most often defined in contrast to Modernism. Where modernism is positivist, riddled with metanarratives, and celebrates Western society, Postmodernism turns a critical eye on Western society and returns to Dadaist ridicule of power structures. The movement is characterized by irony, fragmentation, the blurring of high and low culture, subverting the concepts of authenticity and originality, and an emphasis on image and spectacle. Postmodernism rejects totalizing narratives and absolute truths, favoring the experience of the individual and the minority. It embraces the subjectivity of the viewer to change not just the meaning but the existence of the art itself.

Feminism in Art

In each of these movements, women have had to fight for their place in the gallery and in history. I will be doing additional research on major feminist figures across the several movements, but two figures who already stand out are Hannah Hoch and Barbara Kruger. Hoch’s focus was on dismantling the idea of the “new woman” in the early twentieth century by tearing apart and reassembling advertising and popular imagery of women; this practice was anti-capitalist, anarchist, and feminist in its rejection of what women were being sold as feminine. Kruger similarly focused on advertising and the sale of womanhood through the destruction and collage of marketing materials. My focus would be on the shifting of gender roles, with special focus on LGBTQ topics, and the barriers for non-male, non-heterosexual people as enforced and subverted by technology.
